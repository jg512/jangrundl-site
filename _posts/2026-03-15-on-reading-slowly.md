I read epic fantasy slowly. Not because I'm a slow reader, but because the books I like reward it. You can rush through a *Malazan* novel the way you can rush through a city you've never visited, but you'll miss most of what makes it worth the trip.

## Trust the author for a while

The thing people warn you about with *Gardens of the Moon* is that it drops you in with no explanation. Names, gods, and history arrive all at once, and nobody stops to fill you in. Some readers bounce off it for exactly that reason. I think it's the best thing about the book. It treats you like an adult who can hold a few unanswered questions in your head and keep going.

There's something familiar about that feeling. It's the same one you get reading a large codebase for the first time. Nothing is labelled for your benefit. You have to build the map yourself.

## What a good world has in common with good software

A well-built secondary world and a well-built system share a quality I find hard to name. Everything seems to exist for its own reasons, not for yours. The history was there before you arrived. The pieces fit because someone thought carefully about how they connect, not because they were arranged to be convenient.

The shelf on my home page is mostly books that have that quality. *The Dwarves*, the *Monarchies of God*, the old Conan stories. Worlds that feel like they were lived in before I showed up.

And then, when my eyes are tired, a webtoon. Balance in all things.
